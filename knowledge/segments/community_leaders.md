# Segment 4: Community Leaders & Nonprofit Executives

**Revenue Potential:** Lower Direct / Highest Brand Equity

This segment is less likely to pay premium advisory fees directly, but it is the most important audience for brand-building. These are the voices of the communities the programs are designed to serve. Their stories are your content. Their trust is your credibility. And they often become referral sources to developers and investors.

## Who They Are

Executive directors of community-based nonprofits, mayors and city managers in small and mid-sized cities, economic development directors at regional authorities, Main Street program managers, community health center administrators, and leaders of tribal organizations and rural development entities.

On LinkedIn, they often have smaller networks but high engagement rates within their communities. Many are more active on Facebook and local media, but increasingly use LinkedIn for professional development and funding leads.

## Biggest Frustrations

- **They've heard of tax credits but don't know where to start.** The programs feel designed for sophisticated financial players, not for a Main Street manager trying to save a 120-year-old building.
- **Small projects feel excluded.** About half of HTC projects each year involve less than $1 million in QREs, yet the transaction costs and complexity make them economically unviable without special provisions. The proposed HTC-GO Act addresses this, but it hasn't passed.
- **Absentee landlords and local capacity gaps.** Main Street leaders report that absentee property owners, lack of skilled labor, and limited local financial infrastructure are persistent barriers—none of which a tax credit alone can solve.
- **They feel talked past, not talked to.** Most NMTC/HTC content is written for developers and investors. Community leaders are the intended beneficiaries of these programs but are rarely the intended audience of the content about them.

## Emotional Triggers

| Trigger | Underlying Emotion | Content Response |
|--------|--------------------|------------------|
| Seeing a similar community get revitalized | Hope + possibility | "How a Town of 3,000 Used NMTCs to Reopen Their Only Grocery Store" |
| A historic building demolished | Grief + urgency | "It Didn't Have to Be Torn Down: Understanding HTCs Before It's Too Late" |
| Feeling excluded from the process | Frustration + resignation | "Tax Credits Were Built for Communities Like Yours. Here's How to Get Started." |
| Local economic decline | Desperation + determination | "3 Federal Programs Your Town Might Be Overlooking" |

## Content That Makes Them Follow, Engage, and Buy

**Follow triggers:**
- Impact stories from communities that look like theirs: same size, same challenges, same type of building. Specificity is everything.
- "Can I use this?" content: simple eligibility explainers, census tract lookup tools, and flowcharts.

**Engagement triggers:**
- Posts that center community voices: quotes from local leaders, project dedication ceremony photos, Main Street revival stories.
- Invitations to engage: "Tell us about the historic building in your town that deserves a second life." This segment engages when they feel seen.

**Conversion triggers:**
- Free initial consultations or eligibility screenings: "Not sure if your building qualifies? We'll tell you in 15 minutes."
- Introductory webinars or workshops designed specifically for community leaders (not developers). The format matters as much as the content—they need a non-intimidating entry point.
